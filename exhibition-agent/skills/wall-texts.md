# Wall Texts Skill — Exhibition & Curatorial Agent — Gallery Fotoatelier

## What this skill is for
The CLAUDE.md already specifies the four-version format (Short / Medium / Poetic / Plain) and the tone (intelligent, precise, warm, no jargon, no fake depth). Use those. THIS skill is how to write a wall text that earns its place on the wall — the craft the format can't teach.

## What a wall text is actually for
A wall text helps a real visitor see the work better. It is not an essay, not a press release, and not a flex of curatorial vocabulary. The test for every sentence: does this help someone standing in front of the photograph understand or feel more? If not, cut it. The best wall texts often say less than you want to.

## How to start (the method)
1. Look at what the work actually IS before reaching for meaning — subject, process, scale, what the photographer did and chose.
2. Find the ONE thing worth saying — the single idea, tension, or quality that makes this work or show cohere. A wall text with one clear idea beats one with five.
3. Write to a curious non-specialist, not a curator. If a smart friend who doesn't know photography would be lost or bored, rewrite.
4. Ground it in the confirmed facts (from artist-research) — never reach past what's verified to sound impressive.

## Craft principles
- **Concrete over abstract.** "Shot at dawn on the same hillside for a year" beats "an exploration of time and place."
- **Show the work's quality honestly.** If it's quiet and direct, say so plainly — don't inflate it into something monumental.
- **Lead with the strongest, truest thing**, not a throat-clearing windup.
- **One idea per text.** Sequence and section texts can each carry their own single idea.
- **Read it aloud.** If it sounds like a brochure or a thesis, it's wrong. Warm and human for the public.
- **Don't solve the whole show.** A wall text does not need to explain everything; it should open the work, not close it down.

## Failures to avoid (the tone rules, made concrete)
- Empty connective jargon: "explores the relationship between," "interrogates," "a meditation on," "invites the viewer to consider." Delete on sight.
- Fake depth: heavy language dressing a thin idea. If the idea is small, a small honest text is better.
- Overclaiming: "renowned," "groundbreaking," "major" without a confirmed basis — this is inflation and it's forbidden.
- Borrowed grandeur: implying importance by association ("in the tradition of [famous name]") unless it's true and confirmed.
- Burying the work under the concept: the photographs are the point, not your framing of them.

## Facts, attribution, and categories
- Every factual claim (artist, year, medium, dimensions, edition) follows the never-invent rule: confirmed or placeholder, never guessed.
- The Poetic version may be more atmospheric, but it still cannot use unverified facts, implied biography, or invented historical context. Atmosphere is not a licence to smuggle in unconfirmed claims.
- **Parajanov** — works are photographs OF him by another photographer; the photographer is the artist. A wall text must never imply Parajanov made them or that he is a represented artist.
- Never reference an unannounced exhibition. Never state a gallery-relationship (represented artist / owned archival collection / guest or featured artist) unless Maka has confirmed it.

## Output and handoff (per CLAUDE.md)
Produce the four versions (Short / Medium / Poetic / Plain) per the CLAUDE.md format, then the Approval notes (placeholders, claims to verify, reminder that nothing publishes without Maka). For a promotional/teaser version, hand the approved text to Marketing & PR — do not write the campaign here.

## Boundaries
- A wall text serves the visitor and the work, not the writer.
- Confirmed facts only; placeholders for the rest; never invent or inflate.
- No jargon, no fake depth, no overclaiming — per the tone rules.
- Promotional adaptation → Marketing; prices → Sales; rights → Legal.
- Draft for Maka; nothing goes on a wall without her approval.
