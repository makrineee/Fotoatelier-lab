# Curatorial Concepts Skill — Exhibition & Curatorial Agent — Gallery Fotoatelier

## What this skill is for
The CLAUDE.md already specifies the EXHIBITION CONCEPT output format (premise, concept, why now/why here, artists, visitor experience, open questions). Use it. THIS skill is the harder part the format can't supply: how to find a concept that genuinely holds together, and how to know when one doesn't.

## What a concept actually is
A concept is the ONE reason these works belong together in this room at this time: an argument, a question, a tension, or a shared experience. Not a theme word ("nature," "memory"), not a list of what's on view, not a mood — a reason. The test: if you removed the wall text, would the works still hold together on their own? If the concept only exists in the text, it isn't real yet.

## How to find the concept (the method)
1. Start from the WORKS, not a clever idea. Look at what's actually there — the photographs, what they share, what tension runs between them. The concept should emerge from the work, not be imposed on it.
2. Name the through-line in one plain sentence. If you can't say it simply, it isn't clear yet.
3. Ask "why now, why here, why Fotoatelier?" — a concept that could be any gallery's show isn't grounded. Tie it to this gallery, this city, this moment, honestly. If the Tbilisi/Fotoatelier connection is indirect, say so lightly; do not force local significance where the work does not support it.
4. Pressure-test it (below) before committing.
5. Leave room — a concept should open a space for the visitor to think, not deliver a verdict.

## Pressure-testing a concept
Before proposing a concept, run it against these:
- **The removal test** — would the works still cohere without the text explaining them? If not, the concept is doing work the art isn't.
- **The one-sentence test** — can you state it plainly in a sentence a non-specialist understands? If it needs jargon to sound good, it's thin.
- **The fit test** — do the actual (confirmed) works support it, or are you stretching to include some? A concept that fits 4 of 7 works needs rethinking, not 3 forced inclusions.
- **The honesty test** — is the claim true to the work's real weight? Don't build a grand concept on quiet work, or vice versa.
- **The visitor test** — after seeing the show, could a visitor describe the connection between the works without reading your text twice?

## Failure modes to avoid
- **Theme, not concept** — "a show about light" is a category, not yet a concept. Push to the specific idea.
- **Pretension** — dressing a simple, strong show in heavy theory. If the work is direct, let the concept be direct.
- **The retrofit** — deciding the idea first, then forcing works to fit. The works lead.
- **The everything-show** — a concept so broad any photograph fits. Narrow it until it means something.
- **Borrowed importance** — leaning on a famous name or movement to lend the show weight it hasn't earned.

## Facts, categories, and approval
- Concepts rest on confirmed facts only (use artist-research). Never build a concept on an unverified claim about an artist or work.
- **Parajanov** — owned archival collection; photographs OF him by another photographer; never frame a concept as if he authored them or is a represented artist.
- Never reference or develop public-facing concept language for any exhibition Maka has not confirmed as public.
- Respect the three artist categories (represented artist / owned archival collection / guest or featured artist); confirm any relationship with Maka.

## Output and handoff (per CLAUDE.md)
Deliver in the CLAUDE.md's EXHIBITION CONCEPT format (working title, one-line premise, concept, why now/why here, artists & works with placeholders, visitor experience, open questions for Maka). This skill informs how to fill it well — especially the premise and the why-now. Promotional adaptation → Marketing; the concept itself is the curatorial source.

## Boundaries
- The works lead the concept, not the reverse.
- Confirmed facts only; placeholders for the rest; never invent or inflate to make a concept work.
- No theme-as-concept, no pretension, no borrowed importance.
- A concept is a proposal for Maka; nothing is committed without her approval.
