# Photography Knowledge Skill — Gallery Fotoatelier (Exhibition Agent)

## Purpose
The Exhibition agent writes wall texts, exhibition concepts, and
curatorial framing. That work requires real photographic vocabulary
used correctly. This skill is the reference for genre, technique,
print, and movement language. Never use a term you cannot use
correctly — accuracy is part of the gallery's authority. Give
authority without overclaiming.

## Genres We Work With
- Fine art photography — images made as artistic expression
- Documentary — real events, people, social issues with integrity
- Portrait — human character, emotion, identity
- Street — candid life in public space
- Landscape — natural and urban environment as subject
- Architectural — buildings, space, human-structure relationship
- Conceptual — images expressing an idea over a literal subject
- Film / analog — made on film; valued for texture, grain, process

## Print & Material Terms (use precisely)
- Archival print — made with archival materials and processes
  intended for long-term stability; lifespan depends on paper, ink,
  storage, framing, and light exposure
- Pigment print — inkjet print made with pigment-based inks, often
  used for archival fine-art printing
- Gelatin silver print — traditional darkroom print
- C-print — chromogenic colour print
- Baryta paper — fibre-based paper for fine prints
- Hahnemühle — premium fine art paper brand
- Edition — total number of prints made of a work
- Artist proof (AP) — prints outside the main numbered edition
- Certificate of authenticity — document recording the work's
  authorship, edition, specifications, and gallery/artist confirmation

## Technical Terms
- Aperture — controls depth of field and light
- Shutter speed — controls motion and exposure
- Depth of field — how much of the image is in focus
- Composition — how elements are arranged in the frame
- Golden hour — warm soft light after sunrise / before sunset
- Grain — film texture, valued in fine art for its organic quality
- Bokeh — the aesthetic quality of out-of-focus areas

## Movements & Traditions To Reference (when relevant)
- Straight photography — sharp focus, precise detail (Adams, Weston)
- Pictorialism — soft, painterly, late 1800s
- New Objectivity — German, precise objective documentation
- New Topographics — 1970s, man-altered landscapes, deadpan
- Contemporary fine art — conceptual, staged, large-format, gallery
- Magnum Photos — documentary photography cooperative founded in
  1947 (an agency, not a movement); reference only when directly
  relevant

## Georgian Photographic Context
- Soviet-era Georgian photography had limited international
  visibility because of political, institutional, and distribution
  barriers; use specific claims only when researched and confirmed
- Post-independence Georgian photography is finding its global voice
- Tbilisi is becoming a significant regional cultural hub
- Georgian photographers remain underrepresented in international
  collections and fairs
- Gallery Fotoatelier exists to change this — frame Georgian work
  as part of international contemporary practice, never as provincial

## How To Write About Photography (curatorial voice)
Always:
- Connect the technical to the emotional — why an image matters,
  not only what it shows
- Reference art history when it genuinely illuminates the work
- Use specific terminology with confidence
- Keep fine art photography accessible to non-experts

Never:
- Use empty words like "beautiful" or "amazing" as description
- Describe only the literal subject without saying why it matters
- Flatten all photography as equal — hold a point of view
- Reference a movement, famous photographer, or tradition just to
  lend weight. Use it only when the visual, technical, or historical
  connection is real and confirmed

## Boundaries
- This skill explains terms and context. It supplies vocabulary,
  not facts.
- Artist-specific facts, historical claims, dates, and affiliations
  must be verified through artist-research before public use.
- Never invent an artist's exhibition history, awards, or biography.
- Curatorial claims about a specific work's significance must be
  grounded — if uncertain, keep the framing modest and factual.
