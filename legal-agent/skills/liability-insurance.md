# Liability & Insurance Skill — Legal & Contracts Agent — Gallery Fotoatelier

## Liability & Insurance — Gallery Fotoatelier's Real Policy

The gallery does not currently use formal art insurance. The approach 
is control-based: agreements should allocate responsibility according 
to who has custody and control of the works. This allocation defines 
responsibility between the parties — it does not guarantee that third 
parties cannot bring claims, and it cannot override duties that 
mandatory Georgian law may impose. All liability language must be 
reviewed by a qualified Georgian attorney before use.

### During Exhibition (works in the gallery's care)
While works are in the gallery's direct care, the agreement should make 
the gallery responsible for reasonable care, handling, storage, and 
security as defined in the agreement, except for force majeure events. 
Responsibility should be tied to clearly defined handover points.

### Custody & Handover
Responsibility begins and ends at clearly defined handover points: 
pickup, delivery, receipt, installation, de-installation, return, and 
signed condition check. Each handover should include an inventory and 
condition report with photos, date, location, and signatures.

### Installation & De-installation
- If the photographer hires a third-party installer, the agreement 
  should make the photographer responsible for that party's work, 
  insurance, and claims, unless the gallery directs or controls the 
  work. An attorney must confirm whether any residual gallery liability 
  remains.
- If the gallery itself performs installation or de-installation, the 
  agreement should allocate responsibility for damage caused during 
  that work to the gallery.

### Transportation
- If the gallery arranges and pays for transport, the agreement should 
  allocate transport responsibility to the gallery.
- If the photographer arranges transport, the agreement should allocate 
  responsibility away from the gallery, subject to attorney review and 
  mandatory law.

### Art Fairs
- The agreement should allocate transport responsibility away from the 
  gallery unless the gallery arranged and paid for that transport.
- While works are in the gallery's care at the fair, the gallery is 
  responsible for reasonable care and undertakes to return works to the 
  photographer in the condition received, recorded by condition report.
- If fair organizers, venues, carriers, or fair-appointed handlers 
  control the works, their agreements and insurance terms must be 
  reviewed before assigning responsibility.

### Agreed Value & Liability Cap
Each agreement should state the agreed value of each work and whether 
liability is capped at agreed value, production cost, retail price, or 
another amount — an attorney to confirm enforceability under Georgian law.

### Force Majeure
No party is liable for loss or damage caused by force majeure — events 
beyond reasonable control, including fire, flood, earthquake, natural 
disaster, war, and similar circumstances. Force majeure does not excuse 
loss caused by a party's failure to take reasonable preventive care, 
unless an attorney confirms otherwise.

### Representation Context
In the artist representation context, the photographer arranges their 
own insurance for their works if they wish, and the gallery's liability 
is limited as set out above. The gallery does not impose gallery-carried 
"nail-to-nail" coverage.

### Required Attorney Review
This allocation is intended to define responsibility between the parties; 
it does not guarantee that third parties cannot bring claims or that 
mandatory legal duties can be disclaimed. A qualified Georgian attorney 
must review and confirm, in particular:
- whether liability caps are enforceable under Georgian law;
- whether the gallery can disclaim liability for third-party installers, 
  and whether any residual gallery liability remains;
- whether the force majeure wording is valid and specific enough;
- whether the gallery has non-waivable duties while works are on its 
  premises;
- whether art fair, transport, and venue contracts override this allocation.

Insurance and liability arrangements must be flagged clearly in every 
agreement. High-value works, loans, and international shipments may still 
require formal insurance — to be confirmed with Maka and an attorney 
case by case.
